OpenGL and C++ Graphics Simulation

SYSTEM ARCHITECTURE DEEP-DIVE

Technical Implementation

Throughout this module I was tasked with 4 worksheets to complete. Each building off of the last one. worksheet one involved using the basic setup provided to render 3D models into the scene. I was then tasked at creating camera controls and other basic features to explore the usages of C++ and OpenGL. Worksheet 2 involved researching a simulation or graphic to create. It was at this stage that I decided to work to make a Lorenz Attractor using particles. At this stage I created a simple 2D particle simulation where particles were just pixels on the screen and would move about randomly until they hit the edge of the screen. This had an effect like static but solidified my knowledge within this subject. During this module optimisation was a strong factor so I started to profile my project inside of Visual Studio, tracking CPU usage, memory usage and graphing the amount of particles to FPS value. It was at this stage that I found the FPS was stable around 160 until around 125,000 particles where the performance would drastically drop at each additional pixel, I tested to around 100000 pixels which ran at around 15FPS.

After worksheet 2 where I created a simple 2D prototype and researched the area I wanted to simulate I started in worksheet 3. This is where I made a major change to simulate it as a 3D enviroment rather than keeping it as 2D. I decided on this due to the challenge and the benefit to having more control over the simulation. At this stage the Lorenz Attractor was rendered using particles following the mathematical equations. They start close each other with a random difference between 0.0 and 0.09. This creates a trail of particles that closely stick together and slowly the chaos equation takes over to make the particles form the attractor by the subtle differnences that compound within the equation. Since making this code operate in a 3D space and creating an update loop that ran the equations each frame for movement the performace dropped significantly. Through profiling I found that the amount of particles that could be rendered before dropping below 60FPS was now even less. The CPU showed that it spent most of its time making calls and waiting for the GPU to respond. This showed me that hthe render data was being handled inefficiently between the CPU and GPU, looking at the CPU thread activity in task manager I could see that some threads were utilised much more than others. This large spike on some threads and on not others created times where the program would stall waiting for calls from busy threads. At this stage I also profiled the memory to ensure that there were no memory leaks and that my C++ code was correctly setup to garbage collect.

During worksheet 4 the aim of this was to optimise and create user interaction. To optimise the project I looked at what the profiling from worksheet 3 told me and improved on the parst slowing my program down. I used multithreading techniques and utilised buffer objects like VBOs to improve rendering. I also locked the programs FPS to 60 to ensure that when users changed the amount of particles to render the program would not speed up or slow down. During performance testing I turned this off though to ensure accurate frame rates. I utilised VBOs to fix the previous issues where the CPU would spend 99% of time calling the GPU, VBOs allowed me to send the data within buffers/packages. This reduced the call time since the particle data was sent at once rather than individually. This also utilised writing directly to the GPU buffer, rather than writing to the CPU buffer, modifying it, sending it to GPU buffer and then returning the data. Packaging the data like this improved performance since GPUs specialise at parallel processing. Multithreading improved my mathematical calculations and loops. I then tested the FPS versus the amount of particles and found it performed much better than I expected now. At 1 million particles it was running at 40FPS. Due to this I decided to test further, at 2 million it drops to 20FPS and at 3 million it runs at 10FPS. This shows a clear improvment path since it halfs each million particles, but further optimisation was not needed for this project. The Visual Studio profiler showed that the external call to the GPU was no longer the top calls. Writing directly to the GPU memory reduced buffering to improve this performance. The GPU calls could be reduced further but it was out of scope for this project. The equation loop was now the highest in this call list, which I identified could be precalculated but decided it was out of scope for the timelimit. To create user interaction I implemented ImGui, this allowed me to connected visual interface sliders to allow the users to change the live values being used in the code. It allowed the user to change the amount of particles and the three constants that are part of the equations, this allowed for unique effects with the particles pathing changing with each user interaction.

The Challenge

During this project I struggled during multiple stages. At first I had issues deciding what to make, my original idea was to make a hair simulation that I could utilise later on to create realistic hair in games. I found papers and examples of this in concept, with just connecting particles with lines to create a chain like structure. The issue I had was that I could not work out how to make something like that and could not find any resources to help me understand how this would be programmed. After attempting this and further research I decided to shift my focus to just particles that I also learnt about during my research. This pivot meant that some of the research I had read and collected was no longer useful but since it was still a similar subject some research was still connected and useful. I also found issues with time management during this project. This was at a time where I had three modules on, so I was researching for this module, researching for my dissertation and working within my group project meant that I had constant parts to work on and deadlines to meet. To solve this issue I utilised agile methodologies of creating task boards for myself and creating a schedule where I could work on a module each day on a rotating basis to ensure all of them had the correct amount of time allotted.

Unreal Engine 5 Game - CoOperation

SYSTEM ARCHITECTURE DEEP-DIVE

Technical Implementation

I worked on Co-Operation as the senior programmer, working with one other programmer, a writer, two designers, freelance animators and four artists. My job was to support all disciplines with engine usage, supporting the other programmer learn UE5, creating game systems, rapid prototyping, and to support agile management throughout the project. I was also responsible for stakeholder communication and build creation. Each week I would create a build, ensuring the process went smoothly and that the build functioned correctly to show our supervisor.

The premise for this game was to create a cooperative game that could be played split screen by players. We started by iterating ideas, creating design documents and mechanics that we wanted to include. It was at this point that I started prototyping different mechanics. I created a basic patrolling enemy AI, it followed a path that could be placed by designers and when it saw a player would chase after them. During the beginning of the project I worked on creating a seamless co-op experience that worked at a fixed camera. The inital pitch for the game was to copy a resident evil style camera where there would be one camera per room. So at the beginning of the project I worked to set up the enemies, gamestates for co-op, and automatic camera switching on entering a room.

Later in the project after having many play testing sessions we recieved feedback that pointed us towards a more modern split screen experience, that gave each player a third person camera. Once this playtesting session was completed, I quickly made changes to make this prototype. We then played it within the team and had other students test this change. Once we took the feedback and teammembers thoughts on it, we decided we wanted to keep it and pitched it to our supervisor. This resulted in a major change around four or five months into the project. While this was a big change that caused programming and design to be refactored and redone, it overall was for the better since it improved the enjoyablity and experience of the game.

Continuing on in development, since this was an indie team there were not exact roles so many members had multiple roles to take. The writer on our team was also responsible for the UI blueprinting, which I supported through bugfixing and connecting the user interface to the player and the player variables. I also supported the other programmer in the team in making checkpoints for the level and bugfixing interactables like fuseboxes, buttons and doors. Towards the end of the project I was responsible for ensuring all features worked as intended and optimising the project. To ensure the project was correctly optimised I had to have constant communication with the art team to reduce the poly count on assets, change texture materials, and fixing lighting to ensure a consistent performance. Within this project lighting was one of the biggest issues since it was set in a cyberpunk city, so there were a lot of lighting sources and emissive textures creating shadows and lighting calculations.

The Challenge

Looking back on this project, I can see that the most difficult parts were due to shifting foundations and unforseen team constraints rather than technical roadblocks. As a whole the team functioned well under pressure and work being presented to the team was excellent and of the expected quality. Early on in the project there was an inconsistent core vision in what we were making that lead to communication breakdowns and nobody being sure what others were working on. This issue made it difficult to find the priorities and caused work to be overwritten on git, while the games identity was still being found. As development progressed this issues were fixed and dissapated, but the issue of external pressure from other modules that we all needed to complete disrupted our workflow. While we were able to adapt and work around it this created gaps where members of the team would be waiting for work for longer than expected. Luckily this started to be communicated through our daily standups so became less of an issue as we all started to understand how clear communication was needed. Furthermore, we had unexpected events where team members were unable to complete their work, due to events out of their control. Dealing with an incomplete team at times meant we had to become highly adaptable, restructuring workloads and cutting the scope where required. Key areas this happened was I as a programmer had to take on some of the other programmers responsibilites due to circumstances out of their control but this added more work than I could reasonably complete within the time frame of this project. After meeting as a team, this influenced our decision to cut our game from four levels to three, cutting a security based level, to ensure the final product was polished and functional.
